# Technical Articles: GRC & Cybersecurity

Two research articles written as part of ICDFA graduation requirements, each examining where governance either failed or needs to act before the next failure happens.


## Article 1: The Invisible Hand Behind the Machines
### How GRC Will Shape the Future of AI

AI is no longer experimental. It is making decisions about who gets a loan, who gets hired, who receives a medical diagnosis, and who gets flagged by a criminal justice system. Most of those decisions happen without meaningful oversight.

This article examines the governance gap at the centre of AI deployment: algorithmic bias, synthetic content, and the absence of accountability frameworks. It argues that GRC professionals are not peripheral to this conversation. They are essential to it. The EU AI Act, GDPR, and emerging regulatory frameworks are creating compliance obligations that organisations are not yet prepared to meet.

**Key cases examined:** racial bias in healthcare algorithms, deepfake proliferation, voice cloning fraud, AI-generated political disinformation.

**Core argument:** Governance does not slow down AI. It determines whether AI causes more harm than good.


## Article 2: The Day the Internet Broke
### GRC Lessons from Major Cyber Incidents

Six of the most consequential cyber incidents in recent history, WannaCry, NotPetya, SolarWinds, Colonial Pipeline, Equifax, and Jaguar Land Rover, analysed through a single lens: not how the attacks worked technically, but why governance allowed them to succeed.

The pattern across all six is consistent. Known vulnerabilities left unpatched. Board-level oversight absent. Supply chain risk unmanaged. Basic controls like MFA missing on critical systems. The attacks were sophisticated in some cases. The governance failures were not.

**Combined impact:** tens of billions in losses, critical infrastructure disrupted, 147 million people's data exposed.

**Core argument:** These were not inevitable. They were the predictable result of treating cybersecurity as an IT problem rather than a governance one.


## Why These Articles Are in This Portfolio

GRC roles require more than the ability to follow a framework. They require understanding why governance matters, recognising failure patterns before they repeat, and communicating risk clearly to people who make decisions.

These articles demonstrate that thinking in writing, which is ultimately what GRC documentation, risk registers, and audit reports demand.
